The Proposal: What Security Detention Means

Government proposition 2025/26:95 introduces a new sanction alongside imprisonment and forensic psychiatric care: security detention. Unlike conventional prison sentences, security detention has no fixed endpoint‚Äîit is indefinite.

How the System Would Work

  • Target group: Recidivists who committed serious violent or sexual crimes and are assessed to pose "serious danger" to others
  • Decision: Court sentences to security detention based on forensic psychiatric assessment of future dangerousness
  • Duration: No time limit. Regular review (every three years according to proposal)
  • Conditions: Stricter than ordinary prison but not as restrictive as forensic psychiatric care
  • Release: Only when court assesses that dangerousness has sufficiently decreased

Justice Minister Str√∂mmer argues the proposal complements existing sanctions and gives courts tools to handle "the most dangerous criminals". The government estimates 20-40 persons annually could be sentenced to security detention‚Äîa small number, but with major symbolic and constitutional significance.

Critics point to fundamental problems: How can future criminality be predicted with sufficient precision to justify lifetime incapacitation? What are the error margins in forensic psychiatric risk assessments? And how are the detainee's rights secured when no one knows when deprivation of liberty will end?

Opposition's Dual Strategy

Centre Party's Frontal Attack

Motion 3901, authored by Ulrika Liljeberg and Centre Party's justice policy group, demands total rejection of the proposition. C's argument is principled and constitutionally grounded:

"Indefinite deprivation of liberty contradicts fundamental legal security principles in the Instrument of Government and the European Convention. Sweden should not copy systems that other democracies abandoned after European Court criticism."

‚Äî From Motion 3901, Centre Party

Centre Party points to Germany and the Netherlands, where similar systems were abolished or severely restricted after European Court judgments. Germany introduced Sicherungsverwahrung (preventive detention) but was forced to reform after ECHR rulings finding the system violated Article 5 (right to liberty). The Netherlands followed the same path.

Instead of security detention, Centre Party demands an expedited investigation of alternative measures: intensified post-release supervision, electronic monitoring, mandatory treatment, and better coordination between correctional services and psychiatry. C argues these tools are both constitutionally safer and possibly more effective.

Green Party's Reform Strategy

Motion 3902 from Ulrika Westerlund and Green Party chooses a softer but potentially more feasible line: partial approval with amendments to Criminal Code Chapter 33.

MP accepts the principle of special sanctions for high-risk criminals but demands stronger legal safeguards:

  • Two-year review instead of three-year (faster release assessment)
  • Automatic court review without detainee needing to apply
  • Strengthened right to defense counsel at each review (state-funded)
  • Clearer criteria for what constitutes "serious danger"
  • Independent expert panel for risk assessments (not only forensic psychiatry)

Green Party's strategy is pragmatic: acknowledge need for tools in extreme cases but build in such strong safeguard mechanisms that abuse risk is minimized. It's a compromise that could potentially attract liberals and individual Moderates concerned about constitutional consequences.

Strategic Analysis: Why Two Different Paths?

Centre Party's and Green Party's different approaches reflect their respective political positions. C targets bourgeois voters who value rule of law and individual freedoms‚Äîa classic liberal position. MP focuses on practical legal security and can appeal to Social Democratic skeptics within S who dislike extreme measures.

Tactically: Two different motions provide opinion space for both positions. If C's total rejection fails, MP's reform proposal can function as fallback. Government is forced to defend on two fronts simultaneously.

Constitutional Minefields

The security detention debate fundamentally concerns three legal frameworks that potentially collide:

1. Instrument of Government (Swedish Constitution)

Chapter 2, ¬ß 8 of the Instrument of Government (RF) establishes that "no one may be subjected to deprivation of liberty or other restriction of freedom of movement without trial". While security detention would be decided by court, the question is whether indefiniteness is compatible with the proportionality principle in RF 2:20-21. The Constitution requires interventions in freedoms and rights to be proportionate to their purpose.

Previously, the Law Council‚Äîgovernment's constitutional advisor‚Äîhas been skeptical of open-ended deprivation of liberty. It remains to be seen whether the Law Council opined on proposition 95.

2. European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)

Article 5 ECHR ("Right to liberty and security") permits deprivation of liberty only under strict conditions. The European Court has in several judgments established that preventive deprivation of liberty‚Äîi.e., based on future dangerousness rather than committed crime‚Äîrequires exceptional justification and strong safeguard mechanisms.

Relevant judgments:

  • M. v. Germany (2009): European Court criticized German preventive detention for insufficient review guarantees
  • James, Wells and Lee v. UK (2012): Criticism of British system with indeterminate sentences for dangerous criminals
  • Bergmann v. Germany (2016): Requirements for regular, meaningful review of preventive deprivation of liberty

Sweden has good chances of defending its proposal if review mechanisms are robust. But European Court jurisprudence sets high requirements‚Äîprecisely the weakness Green Party's motion attempts to address.

3. EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 6 of the EU Charter guarantees the right to liberty and security. While Swedish criminal law questions normally fall outside EU competence, the Charter may become relevant if security detention is applied to EU citizens or if Sweden later must harmonize with EU legislation on free movement.

This is likely the background to the Prime Minister's EU Committee consultation on Monday‚Äîa signal that government is aware of international dimensions.

International Perspectives: What Can Sweden Learn?

Sweden is not alone in grappling with dangerous recidivists. Several countries have experimented with preventive deprivation of liberty‚Äîwith mixed results.

Germany: Reformed After European Court Criticism

Germany introduced Sicherungsverwahrung (preventive detention) as early as 1933, but the system was significantly reformed after several European Court judgments 2009-2011. The problems: insufficient review, lack of treatment during detention, and unclear release criteria.

Germany responded with:

  • Shorter review intervals (annual assessment)
  • Therapeutic focus‚Äîdetainees must be offered intensive treatment
  • Retroactivity ban‚Äîlaw cannot be applied to previously convicted

Despite reforms, the system continues to be criticized for stigmatization and low rehabilitation rates.

Netherlands: From TBS to Limited System

Terbeschikkingstelling (TBS) is the Dutch system for forensic psychiatric care with preventive detention. Like Germany, Netherlands was forced to reform after ECHR criticism. Today TBS is more therapeutically oriented and with clear rehabilitation goals.

Key to Dutch success: strong integration between justice system and psychiatry, plus significant resources for treatment. Sweden has historically weak links between correctional services and psychiatry‚Äîa deficiency that could undermine security detention's legitimacy.

Norway: Forvaring ‚Äì A Nordic Model?

Norway introduced forvaring (preventive detention) in 2002, with similar principles to the Swedish proposal. The system has passed European Court scrutiny, partly thanks to strong review mechanisms and treatment focus.

Norwegian lessons:

  • Transparency in risk assessments‚Äîmethods must be scientifically grounded
  • Treatment, not just detention‚Äîotherwise it becomes sentence enhancement in practice
  • Limited scope‚Äîonly 30-40 persons per year sentenced to forvaring

If Sweden follows Norwegian model, constitutional risks can be reduced. But it requires investments in treatment capacity‚Äîsomething not explicitly mentioned in proposition 95.
