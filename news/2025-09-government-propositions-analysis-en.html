Sweden Lifts Uranium Ban as Defense and Energy Policies Shift

September 15, 2025 | 8 min read

In a dramatic reversal of energy policy, Sweden's government on August 28th proposed lifting the decades-old ban on uranium mining. This is the most controversial of eight propositions submitted to the Riksdag in late August and early September, a package of reforms that together signal a fundamental reassessment of national security, energy independence, and economic resilience. As Sweden adjusts to its new role as a NATO member and confronts rising geopolitical tensions, the government is reshaping policy frameworks that have been in place for generations.

Context: A Nation in Transition

The eight propositions arrive at a critical juncture for Sweden. The country joined NATO in March 2024 after two centuries of military non-alignment, and is now elevating its defense capabilities to match alliance expectations. Simultaneously, the long-term energy transition faces the constraints of reality: wind and solar power alone cannot deliver the baseload power required for industrial competitiveness. The coalition government—composed of the Moderates, Sweden Democrats, Christian Democrats, and Liberals—has managed to deliver a unified agenda across areas that traditionally provoke political division.

That these propositions were submitted nearly simultaneously is neither coincidence nor tactical overkill. It is strategic signaling: Sweden is undergoing a coordinated modernization of its security doctrine, energy infrastructure, and economic control systems. The question is whether the Riksdag, where the opposition controls substantial power in committees, will approve this vision intact.

Defense: From Civil Protection to Civil Defense

Perhaps the most symbolically charged proposals are the two from the Ministry of Defense. Proposition 2024/25:205, submitted on September 4th, proposes renaming the Swedish Civil Contingencies Agency (MSB) to the Agency for Civil Defense. On the surface, it seems trivial—a bureaucratic rebranding. But in Swedish security policy, names are meaningful. The new name places MSB squarely in the defense domain, signaling that contingency planning's primary purpose is no longer natural disasters or peacetime crises, but society's defensibility in war.

Defense Minister Carl-Oskar Bohlin has long argued for a return to "total defense"—the Cold War doctrine where every sector of society is responsible for maintaining its function during military attack. The name change is part of this doctrinal shift. It also reflects the Swedish National Audit Office's (Riksrevisionen) review of civil defense development, which the government responds to in Proposition 2024/25:206. The audit report, published in April 2025, criticized fragmentation in state governance—too many agencies, unclear division of responsibilities, and insufficient resources. The government's response, according to the proposition, promises "clearer leadership" and "strengthened resources," but lacks specific budget commitments, leaving the opposition skeptical.

Critics, particularly from the Left Party and Green Party, argue that militarizing civil contingency agencies risks undermining their grassroots origins and neglecting non-military risks like climate change and pandemics. But for the coalition, the prioritization is clear: NATO's resilience requirements mean Sweden must be able to maintain societal functions under military threats. NATO is now the foundational security framework.

Economic Security: From Corporate Espionage to F-Tax Abuse

The Ministry of Justice has delivered two propositions that balance security against liberty. Proposition 2024/25:208, submitted on September 4th by Justice Minister Gunnar Strömmer, expands criminal liability for attacks on trade secrets. Previous Swedish law focused on direct theft; the new legislation criminalizes unauthorized access, acquisition, and use of trade secrets, aligning with EU Directive 2016/943. The geopolitical context is obvious: as Sweden becomes a technology leader in defense industries, battery technology, and green steel, the incentives for industrial espionage from competing states increase.

Proposition 2024/25:202, submitted August 21st, expands opportunities to serve prison sentences with electronic monitoring (ankle bracelets). This is a pragmatic measure aimed at reducing prison overcrowding and lowering costs while maintaining punitive consequences. The Prison and Probation Service estimates that per-day costs for ankle bracket monitoring are 60% lower than prison detention. But the opposition, particularly Social Democrats, questions whether electronic monitoring can provide the same rehabilitation resources as traditional imprisonment. It is a classic liberal-conservative dilemma: efficiency versus rehabilitation.

From the Ministry of Climate and Enterprise comes Proposition 2024/25:201 (August 21st), which expands grounds for business activity bans. Previously, courts required proof of recidivism risk; the new legislation allows bans based solely on the severity of the crime. Specifically targeting abuse of the F-tax system—where salaried workers falsely register as self-employed to evade taxes—the proposition is part of the government's broader campaign against economic crime. Business groups warn, however, that overly broad powers could penalize legitimate entrepreneurs who make mistakes during business growth.

Energy Independence: Nuclear Power's Return

The centerpiece proposition in this constellation is undoubtedly Proposition 2024/25:203: the lifting of the uranium mining ban. On August 28th, Climate and Energy Minister Romina Pourmokhtari presented this historic reversal, which repeals the 1992 law that banned all prospecting and mining of uranium. The government argues that Sweden must be able to supply its own nuclear fuel to achieve net-zero goals by 2045 while ensuring energy independence. With Europe's largest known uranium reserves (thanks to the Ranstad deposits near Falköping), Sweden could theoretically supply its nuclear reactors for centuries.

The decision is both ideological and practical. Ideologically, it represents a complete rejection of the 1970s and 1980s nuclear skepticism, when Sweden voted to phase out nuclear power after the Chernobyl disaster. Public opinion has shifted: a 2025 SIFO survey shows 62% of Swedes support nuclear power, compared to 38% in 2015. Practically, it acknowledges the reality that wind and solar, despite decades of investment, still require fossil backup or nuclear baseload to ensure grid stability.

But uranium mining is environmentally controversial. The Ranstad deposit sits near Lake Vättern, Sweden's second-largest lake and drinking water source for two million people. The Green Party and Left Party have vowed to fight the proposition, citing risks of groundwater contamination and radioactive waste. The government assures that modern mining technology (in-situ leaching) minimizes surface damage, but critics remain unconvinced. The proposition will be referred to the Environment and Agriculture Committee, where the Greens hold significant influence despite being in opposition.

Complementarily, Proposition 2024/25:199 (August 21st) introduces confidentiality rules for "strategic net-zero projects." Proposed by Deputy Prime Minister Ebba Busch, it grants the government authority to classify certain energy infrastructure projects on national security grounds. The rationale is clear: if Sweden is building nuclear reactors or sensitive green technology, the government wants to prevent foreign adversaries from obtaining detailed construction plans. But transparency advocates warn that broad confidentiality could obscure environmental malpractice or favor politically connected companies. It is the balance between security and democratic accountability—a common theme in this proposition package.

Pension Policy: Flexibility for an Aging Population

Finally, Proposition 2024/25:200 from the Ministry of Finance (August 28th) gives retirees greater flexibility to withdraw pension insurance. Previously, Swedish law required certain private pension plans to be paid out as annuities; the new legislation allows lump-sum withdrawals or customized schedules. For an aging population where many face variable expenses (healthcare, housing), this provides autonomy. Finance Minister Elisabeth Svantesson argues it is fundamental economic freedom. Critics warn, however, that it may tempt some retirees to withdraw too much too early, leaving them vulnerable in old age. Pension system safeguards exist for a reason.

Political Dynamics: Coalition Cohesion and Opposition Challenge

The remarkable aspect of these propositions is the coalition's unity. Typically, uranium mining would split the Moderates and Liberals (with their green leanings), while defense militarization might trouble the Liberals' liberty tradition. But NATO membership and the energy crisis have created consensus. The Sweden Democrats, the coalition's right wing, get their desired defense buildup; the Moderates get economic pragmatism; the Christian Democrats get family pension flexibility; the Liberals get technology security guarantees. It is a soberly negotiated agenda.

The opposition is divided. The Social Democrats, still bruised from the 2022 election defeat, criticize the uranium mining proposition but lack a unified energy plan. The Left Party and Green Party are principled opponents, but together they control only 44 of 349 Riksdag seats. The key battleground will be the committees, where even minor parties can negotiate amendments. The Environment and Agriculture Committee is critical for the uranium mining decision; the Justice Committee will scrutinize electronic monitoring carefully; the Defense Committee, where Social Democrats have strong veterans, will demand proof that the government meets the National Audit Office's critiques.

Public opinion supports nuclear power and defense spending, but is wary of confidentiality and business bans. The geopolitical context—Russia's aggression in Ukraine, China's industrial espionage, the climate clock—favors the government's narrative. But environmental scandals or implementation failures could quickly turn opinion.

Implications: Sweden and the Future

If these propositions pass—and most are expected to, with minor amendments—they will reshape Sweden fundamentally. Energy independence through domestic nuclear power would reduce reliance on continental imports or intermittent renewables. Defensively, a fully integrated civil defense would strengthen NATO's northern flank. Economically, broader security laws and stricter business bans would sharpen Sweden's resilience against transnational crime.

But there are risks. Uranium mining could create environmental disasters if executed carelessly. Excessive confidentiality could undermine democratic trust. And electronic monitoring, if scaled up rapidly, may fail to deliver rehabilitation results. These propositions represent the government's bet that security and pragmatism must trump idealism in a dangerous world. Whether the Riksdag and Swedish public share that assessment will be determined in coming months.

What Comes Next?

The propositions now undergo review in relevant Riksdag committees: Environment and Agriculture for uranium mining; Defense for MSB renaming and civil defense; Justice for corporate espionage and electronic monitoring; Finance for pension flexibility. Public hearings are expected in October, with plenary votes before the Christmas recess. The opposition will amend, but the government's majority means the main outlines will survive. By the new year, Sweden may be on course for a new era—one of nuclear renaissance, militarized civil defense, and strengthened economic security.

Internationally, European capitals are watching closely. If Sweden can successfully balance nuclear expansion with climate goals, it may become a model for other EU members struggling with energy transition. If NATO allies see Sweden's civil defense as a gold standard, it could influence the alliance's broader resilience strategy. Sweden, long known for its cautious neutrality, is now embracing a bolder, more assertive security posture. The coming months will reveal whether that boldness is visionary or reckless.
